How this works: A Poisson model — each team's goals modeled by their goals-per-game rates, adjusted for opponent strength and a small home-field advantage (10%). The Poisson distribution is the standard tool for soccer goals and handles low-scoring randomness well. The model assumes both teams' attacks and defenses are stable — which is mostly true, though it can't account for player injuries, weather, or whether the goalkeeper had a bad burrito at lunch.

What this tells parents

Consistency matters more than peak performance in youth soccer development. A team that wins 4-0 then loses 0-8 is hard to plan around. Our girls are showing the opposite — solid wins with reasonable scorelines, occasional close losses against tougher boys' teams. That's a healthy pattern.

The most volatile teams in the league have margin standard deviations above 7 goals per match — meaning a typical game could be a 4-goal win or a 4-goal loss. The Dragons sit much tighter than that.

Top 10 — Strengths & Weaknesses

Sorted by composite strength score, not league rank
Why this order differs from the standings: The league table ranks by total points, which favors teams that have played more games. To compare relative strength, I'm using a composite score from three rate-based metrics: points per game, goal differential per game, and log-ratio of goals scored to goals conceded. Each is z-scored across active teams and averaged. The number in red on each card is that score — higher is better, and the gap between #1 and #2 (about 0.65) is meaningful.

How to read the profile findings: These are derived from the available numbers — goals scored/conceded, win/draw/loss patterns, margin variance, recent form, and individual match results from the past ~5 weeks. They are not tactical scouting reports. I have no information about formations, individual players, or playing style.
